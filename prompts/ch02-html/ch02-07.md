> **Prompt**: How do search engines use HTML structure to understand a page?

Search engines like Google don't just "read" your text; they use your HTML structure as a **map** to determine what is important, what is secondary, and what the overall "neighborhood" of your content is.

When a search engine crawler (like Googlebot) visits your site, it builds a model of your page based on these structural cues.

---

## 1. Document Hierarchy (The "Outline")
Search engines use heading tags (`<h1>` through `<h6>`) to create an outline of your content. This tells the bot the difference between the main topic and supporting details.

* **`<h1>`**: The "Title" of the page. There should usually only be one.
* **`<h2>` - `<h3>`**: The "Chapters" or sub-topics.
* **The Benefit**: If a user searches for a specific sub-topic, Google can link them directly to that section of your page if it’s properly tagged with an `<h2>`.



---

## 2. Semantic Landmarks
Semantic tags tell the search engine which parts of the page are "meat" and which are "noise."

* **`<main>`**: Tells the crawler, "Ignore the headers and sidebars; the answer to the user's query is right here."
* **`<nav>`**: Helps the crawler discover other pages on your site to index them faster.
* **`<aside>`**: Tells the crawler this info is related but not the primary focus (like "Related Posts").
* **`<footer>`**: Contains "meta" info like copyright and contact details, which helps establish your site's authority (E-E-A-T).

---

## 3. Rich Snippets and Structured Data
While standard HTML is good, search engines look for specific patterns to create "Rich Results" (those fancy boxes with ratings, prices, or recipes).

* **The Structure**: By nesting a `<time>` tag or using `<ul>` for a list of steps, you make it easier for Google to extract a **Featured Snippet**.
* **Example**: If you use `<ol>` for a recipe, Google is more likely to show your page as a step-by-step guide in the search results.



---

## 4. Relationship Links
The way you nest your tags shows the relationship between different pieces of data.

| HTML Structure | Search Engine Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- |
| `<a>` inside `<nav>` | This is a high-priority site-wide link. |
| `<a>` inside `<article>` | This is a contextual "backlink" or reference. |
| `alt` inside `<img>` | This is what this image represents (crucial for Image Search). |
| `<strong>` / `<em>` | These keywords are more important than the surrounding text. |

---

## Why "Div-Soup" Hurts Your SEO
If your entire page is made of `<div>` tags, the search engine sees a "flat" document. It has to work much harder to guess what the title is versus what the footer is. 
* **Risk**: It might accidentally index your "Terms of Service" text as the most important part of the page instead of your actual product description.

---

## Technical Performance (The "Crawl Budget")
Search engines have a "Crawl Budget"—a limited amount of time they spend on your site. 
* **Valid HTML:** Crawlers can fly through valid, well-nested code. 
* **Broken HTML:** If the crawler hits "tangled" or invalid nesting, it may stop indexing that page early to avoid getting stuck in a loop, meaning some of your content never makes it to the search results.
