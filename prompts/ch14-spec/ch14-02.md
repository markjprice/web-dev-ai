> **Prompt**: Please explain why “non-goals” are important in software specifications and give examples from real-world projects.

In software engineering, a **Non-Goal** is a deliberate statement of what a project will **not** do. While the "Goals" section defines the destination, the "Non-Goals" section acts as the guardrails that prevent **Scope Creep**—the gradual, uncontrolled expansion of a project's requirements.

In 2026, as AI-driven development accelerates the pace of coding, Non-Goals are more critical than ever because they provide a "hard stop" for both human developers and AI agents who might otherwise "helpfully" add unnecessary complexity.

---

## 1. Why Non-Goals are Important

### A. Focus and Resource Allocation
Every feature added to a project consumes "innovation tokens" (time, budget, and mental energy). By explicitly stating what you aren't building, you protect the resources needed for the core mission.

### B. Defeating the "While You're At It" Syndrome
Stakeholders often try to attach small, unrelated requests to a moving project. A Non-Goals section allows a Product Manager to say, *"That is a great idea, but it is explicitly listed as a Non-Goal for this sprint to ensure we launch on time."*

### C. Architectural Clarity
If a Non-Goal is "Support for Offline Mode," the engineering team knows they don't need to implement complex local-first syncing logic or Service Workers. This simplifies the initial technical design significantly.



---

## 2. Real-World Examples of Non-Goals

### Example 1: The "MVP" Mobile App (e.g., Early Instagram)
* **Goal:** Allow users to apply filters to photos and share them instantly.
* **Non-Goal:** **Desktop Uploads.** * *Result:* By refusing to build a desktop web uploader, the team stayed laser-focused on the mobile experience and saved months of cross-platform development.

### Example 2: A High-Performance API (e.g., Stripe's early API)
* **Goal:** Provide a reliable, developer-friendly way to process credit cards.
* **Non-Goal:** **End-to-End Encryption (E2EE) for User Data.**
    * *Result:* By making E2EE a non-goal (and instead focusing on PCI-compliant tokenization), they avoided the massive performance overhead and key-management complexity that would have slowed down their primary service.

### Example 3: TallyApp (The Survey Tool)
* **Goal:** Create a document-style form builder that is easy to use.
* **Non-Goal:** **Real-time Collaborative Editing.**
    * *Result:* By deciding *not* to be "Google Docs for Forms" in Version 1, the developers avoided the nightmare of Operational Transformation (OT) or CRDT logic, allowing them to ship the core builder a year earlier.

---

## 3. How to Write Effective Non-Goals

Good Non-Goals are **specific** and **temporary**. They aren't "Never-Goals"; they are "Not-Right-Now-Goals."

| Poor Non-Goal | Professional Non-Goal |
| :--- | :--- |
| "The app won't be slow." | "Optimizing for sub-100ms latency is a non-goal; 500ms is acceptable for MVP." |
| "No Android version." | "Native Android support is a non-goal for Q1; we will target iOS and Mobile Web only." |
| "No complex features." | "Automated data migration from Typeform is a non-goal for this release." |



---

## 4. The "Invisible" Benefit: AI Alignment
When using AI coding assistants like **Claude Code** or **GitHub Copilot**, including a "Non-Goals" section in your prompt prevents the AI from importing massive libraries or writing boilerplate for features you don't want. 

**Example Prompt Fragment:**
> "...Non-Goal: Do not implement OAuth2; we are using simple Session-based Auth only for this phase."

Without that Non-Goal, the AI might see the word "Authentication" and spend 200 tokens generating a complex, unnecessary OAuth configuration that you then have to spend time deleting.
