> **Prompt**: Why do AI coding assistants tend to make incorrect assumptions when specifications are incomplete? Describe the risks of vague requirements when using AI coding assistants.

AI coding assistants—including the advanced models of 2026—do not possess "intuition" in the human sense. When they encounter a gap in your specifications, they don't stop to ask for clarification unless specifically prompted to do so. Instead, they fill those gaps using **probabilistic inference**.

This behavior is the root cause of "hallucinated logic" and architectural mismatches.

---

## 1. Why AI Makes Incorrect Assumptions
The primary reason for incorrect assumptions is the **"Path of Least Resistance"** in statistical modeling.

* **Statistical Frequency vs. Specific Intent:** If your prompt is vague, the AI defaults to the most common patterns in its training data. For example, if you ask for a "user login," the AI will assume a standard email/password flow even if your project strictly requires Biometric or OAuth-only authentication.
* **The "Completion" Bias:** LLMs are trained to finish the text. If a requirement is missing (e.g., "how should the system handle a database timeout?"), the AI will invent a plausible-sounding error handler rather than leaving a "TODO" or asking you how your specific infrastructure handles retries.
* **Context Window Limitations:** Even with 1M+ token windows, the AI's "attention" is finite. If a specification is buried or poorly defined, the model may prioritize its internal "knowledge" of a framework (like Django or React) over the subtle nuances of your specific business logic.



---

## 2. The Risks of Vague Requirements
Using vague requirements with an AI assistant creates a "compounding error" effect. Small misunderstandings at the start of a prompt lead to massive technical debt in the generated code.

### A. The "Black Box" Security Risk
When requirements are vague, AI often prioritizes **functionality over security**.
* **The Risk:** If you don't specify "No PII logging," the AI might include a helpful-looking logger that saves raw user passwords or credit card tokens to a local text file just because that's how "simple" tutorials in its training data were written.

### B. Architectural Mismatch (The "Square Peg" Problem)
AI often assumes a "standard" architecture that may conflict with your existing codebase.
* **The Risk:** You might be building a **Microservices** app, but a vague prompt leads the AI to generate **Monolithic** code with hardcoded dependencies. By the time you realize the logic doesn't scale, you've already integrated hundreds of lines of incompatible code.

### C. Logic "Drift" and Silent Failures
This is the most dangerous risk because the code **looks correct** and **runs without errors**, but it calculates the wrong result.
* **Example:** In a survey app like TallyApp, if you don't specify how to handle "Multi-choice" data (e.g., "Comma-separated string" vs "JSON list"), the AI will pick one. If your frontend expects a list but the AI-generated backend sends a string, the app will fail silently or crash in production under heavy load.

## 3. Comparison: Vague vs. Precise Requirements

| Feature | Vague Prompt | Precise Prompt |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Logic** | "Make a payment page." | "Create a Stripe Checkout integration using the `PaymentIntent` API and a 15-minute expiration." |
| **Error Handling** | Generic `try/except`. | "Log errors to Sentry; retry 3 times with exponential backoff for 503 errors." |
| **Data Flow** | Assumes standard DB. | "Use a Redis cache for session data and Postgres for long-term survey results." |
| **Result** | High hallucination risk. | High reliability and "copy-paste" readiness. |

---

## 4. How to Prevent "Assumptive Coding"
To stop the AI from guessing, you must **close the loop** using structured prompting:

1.  **Define the "Negative Space":** Tell the AI what *not* to do (e.g., "Do not use any third-party libraries for authentication").
2.  **Use the "Verification Gate":** End your prompt with: *"Before generating code, list any assumptions you are making about my infrastructure or data schema."*
3.  **Provide a "Reference implementation":** If you have a specific way of writing views, paste one example so the AI mimics your style rather than its training data.
