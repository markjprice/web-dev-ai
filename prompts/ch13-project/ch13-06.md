> **Prompt**: Why does asking the AI to restate requirements before generating code reduce hallucinations? Please explain the psychology behind structured prompting and why it improves code quality.

Asking an AI to restate requirements before generating code is effectively forcing the model to perform **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** reasoning. In the world of Large Language Models (LLMs), this is less about "memory" and more about **computational alignment**.

By making the AI "show its work" first, you are ensuring that its internal probability map for the next token is anchored to your specific constraints rather than a generic pattern it found in its training data.

---

## 1. The Psychology of "Chain-of-Thought"
LLMs generate text one token at a time based on statistical likelihood. If you ask for code immediately, the model might "rush" toward a common solution it has seen thousands of times (like a generic Django view), even if your requirements specify something unique.

* **Explicit Attention:** When a model restates requirements, it must process every word of your prompt and regenerate it. This places those specific constraints into the **KV Cache** (the model's short-term working memory). 
* **The "Slow Thinking" Effect:** Similar to Daniel Kahneman’s *System 1 vs. System 2* thinking, a direct prompt triggers a "System 1" response (fast, intuitive, pattern-matching). Forcing a restatement triggers a "System 2" response (deliberate, logical, and structured).



---

## 2. Why it Reduces Hallucinations
Hallucinations often happen when the model loses the "thread" of a complex prompt.

* **Anchor Points:** If the AI restates that "the survey must be anonymous and exclude IP logging," it has created a text-based "anchor." When it subsequently writes the code, the most "statistically probable" next step is to follow the rules it just typed out.
* **Conflict Resolution:** If your prompt contains a contradiction (e.g., "Make it anonymous but show me who sent it"), the act of restating usually forces the AI to catch the error. It might say, *"I will create an anonymous survey, but I noticed you asked to see the sender; I will treat this as a semi-anonymous structure."* This prevents the code from being a broken hybrid of two ideas.

---

## 3. Impact on Code Quality
Restating requirements shifts the model's objective from **"Generating Code"** to **"Solving the Requirements."**

* **Contextual Precision:** Instead of using a generic boilerplate, the AI is more likely to use the specific variable names, database fields, and logic flows it just listed in the restatement.
* **Verification Loop:** It gives *you* a chance to intervene. If the AI restates the requirements and misses a critical security constraint, you can stop it before it spends tokens generating 200 lines of insecure code.

---

## Structured Prompting vs. Zero-Shot
Structured prompting (like the **"Restate-then-Code"** method) creates a logical bridge that generic prompting lacks.

| Prompting Style | Probability of Success | Primary Risk |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Zero-Shot** ("Write a Django app...") | Moderate | Model fills gaps with "hallucinated" defaults. |
| **CoT / Restatement** ("First, list the models...") | **High** | Slightly higher token usage/cost. |
| **Few-Shot** (Providing examples) | Very High | Model may over-index on your examples. |

---

## Example: The "Verification Gate"
If you use a prompt like:
> "First, summarize my security requirements for this anonymous survey. Then, write the Django `models.py`."

The AI is forced to write out "No IP storage" and "No PII" first. As it begins the code, the "attention" mechanism is heavily weighted toward those phrases, making it statistically unlikely to include an `ip_address = models.GenericIPAddressField()` line in the model.
