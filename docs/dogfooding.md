# Dog fooding

“Eat your own dog food” is a blunt expression from the software industry that means: use the product you build. If you create a tool, platform, framework, or service, you should rely on it yourself in real, everyday work. If it is awkward, buggy, slow, or confusing, you will discover that quickly. If it is solid, you gain confidence and credibility.

The phrase is intentionally unglamorous. Dog food is not aspirational. It is ordinary, functional, and assumed to meet a basic standard. The underlying message is simple: if you expect customers to depend on something, you should be willing to depend on it too.

## Origins and engineering culture at Microsoft

The idea predates the exact wording. In the 1970s and 1980s, technology companies increasingly emphasized internal use of their own hardware and software. One of the earliest widely cited uses of the specific phrase comes from Microsoft in the late 1980s. Paul Maritz reportedly sent an internal memo in 1988 encouraging teams to “eat our own dog food” by using Microsoft products internally before release. The concept became formalized as “dogfooding.”

By the 1990s, dogfooding was common vocabulary across engineering teams at Microsoft, Netscape, and later Google. Internal builds were used company-wide before public release. That forced developers, product managers, and executives to experience the same friction customers would face. It surfaced usability flaws that would not appear in a lab. It exposed performance bottlenecks. It made defects embarrassing in a productive way.

Engineers liked the phrase precisely because it was unpolished. It emphasized humility and accountability. If the build breaks your own workflow, that is not a marketing problem. It is a product problem.

## What dogfooding actually enforces

Dogfooding does three important things in engineering culture:

1. First, it collapses distance between creator and user. You stop designing for an abstract “end user” and start designing for yourself as a real operator.

2. Second, it forces quality under realistic conditions. Internal users behave unpredictably and impatiently. They push the product in ways documentation never anticipates.

3. Third, it creates organizational alignment. When executives, sales staff, and support teams rely on the same tools, product shortcomings become visible across the company.

The phrase works because it is uncomfortable. You are not “sampling” your product. You are living on it.

## “Drink your own champagne” and how it misses the point

“Drink your own champagne” emerged later as a softer alternative used by marketing departments. It implies that your product is luxurious and delightful. Champagne is celebratory. It suggests success and refinement.

But that shift subtly changes the meaning.

Champagne is aspirational. Dog food is baseline sustenance. The original metaphor is about obligation and proof. The revised metaphor is about prestige and image.

Engineers used “eat your own dog food” to signal discipline. Marketers often prefer “drink your own champagne” because it sounds polished and brand-friendly. The problem is that it sanitizes the idea. It turns a culture of accountability into a branding exercise.

If you are “drinking champagne,” you are showcasing something glamorous. If you are “eating dog food,” you are stress-testing something functional. Those are different postures.

## Why the blunt version matters

The strength of the original phrase is that it carries mild self-mockery. It acknowledges that internal builds can be rough. It assumes imperfection. It implies shared responsibility for improvement.

The champagne version flatters the product. The dog food version challenges it.

In practice, the companies that benefited most from dogfooding treated it as a hard engineering requirement, not a slogan. Internal email ran on pre-release builds. Browsers were dogfooded across the company. Cloud platforms hosted internal tools before external customers ever touched them.

The original phrase survived because it reinforced a simple rule: if you are not willing to rely on your own product, you should not expect anyone else to.

Engineers popularized it. When it gets rebranded into something slick and self-congratulatory, it loses its teeth. The original metaphor was never meant to be pretty. It was meant to enforce integrity.
